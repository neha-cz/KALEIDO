# KALEIDO

Simulating psychedelic cognition in LLM inference. 

## Mech Interp Findings

We tested whether the entropic brain hypothesis (EBH) — which holds that psychedelic-like cognitive states correspond to elevated entropy of neural dynamics — maps onto transformer inference, using attention inverse-temperature β as the interventional analog of landscape flattening under the modern Hopfield interpretation of attention. Flattening β on early layers (2–3) of Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct produced a large, reliable behavioral effect: local coherence collapsed (perplexity rose from ≈3.7 to ≈19.5) while the model's output remained non-degenerate, consistent with a "loosened" generative regime. However, a sequence of mechanistic interventions showed that this effect is not mediated by an increase in entropy. A necessity/sufficiency dissociation found that a structured perturbation of the attention output (value re-weighting), applied with attention-weight entropy held fixed at baseline, reproduced roughly three-quarters of β's behavioral effect, whereas raising attention-weight entropy while restoring the output toward baseline reproduced almost none (~5%). The behavioral effect is therefore carried by structured value-mixing propagating through the residual stream, with attention-weight entropy a co-occurring bystander rather than the causal driver.

We then asked whether a different entropy index might be the correct EBH analog, screening three further measures drawn from the EBH literature. On the residual stream — where the value-mixing result predicts the disturbance lives — we computed Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZc) of binarized activation trajectories and a spatial-complexity measure (across-unit activation entropy), the latter being the more robust of the two as it requires no temporal-axis assumption. Neither tracked the behavioral effect: across both the intervened layers and the network-wide mean, β's large coherence collapse was accompanied by negligible movement in spatial entropy (≈0.0–0.16% change) and a small decrease in LZc (≈4–8%). We additionally ported the measure of Viol et al. (2017) — the Shannon entropy of the degree distribution of a functional-connectivity graph, which increases under ayahuasca — by treating the attention matrix as a token-token connectivity graph and computing degree-distribution entropy alongside its companion integration metrics (clustering, global efficiency), comparing baseline and β at matched edge density to avoid threshold artifacts. This was the one index that moved substantially, but it moved in the wrong direction: under β, degree-distribution entropy decreased (by ≈0.51 nats) and clustering also decreased, whereas the psychedelic signature is increased degree entropy with increased local integration. β drives the attention connectivity toward a more regular, lower-entropy structure — the inverse of the entropic-brain pattern.

To furher explore the effects of attention-graph degree-distribution entropy, we directly introduced an intervention independent of β, and asked whether moving it shifts reasoning. Raising degree entropy (broadening the attention graph toward hub structure, +0.2 to +0.43 nats) left reasoning essentially unchanged at every layer: local coherence remained at 1.0 and perplexity stayed near baseline (≈4.0–4.7 vs. 4.1), in contrast to β's collapse to ≈19.5. Driving degree entropy in the opposite direction (toward a regular, low-entropy graph) instead degraded generation to the point of collapse. The effect is thus strongly asymmetric and, critically, the EBH-favorable direction — raising degree entropy, exactly as psychedelics do — produced no cognitive loosening whatsoever. Thus, like attention entropy, degree entropy rising is at most a co-occurring signature, not a driver of altered processing.

In other words, across four distinct entropy/complexity indices — attention-weight entropy, residual-stream LZc, residual-stream spatial entropy, and attention-graph degree-distribution entropy — none rose with the behavioral effect.
